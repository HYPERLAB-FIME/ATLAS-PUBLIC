Abstract

This specification defines the Agent Trust Layer and Assurance Standard (ATLAS) protocol — a chained-capability-token audit framework for agentic commerce. ATLAS standardises how accredited assessor agents obtain bounded, encrypted evidence from shopping agents, evaluate intent fidelity and policy compliance, and produce signed artifacts that can be consumed upstream and downstream without exposing raw conversations or unnecessary personal data.

ATLAS supports two audit-initiation patterns: a holder-mediated path (merchant or credential provider commissions an assessor) and a direct path (shopping agent self-commissions an assessor for compliance or regulatory evidence). Application trust relies on chained JWS/JWT delegation tokens and JWE-encrypted evidence over HTTPS with standard TLS. Downstream payment-rail integration uses compact CBReportToken references assembled into a redeem_tokens field by the merchant processor for ISO 8583, EMV 3-D Secure, and ISO 20022.

1. Introduction§

Agentic commerce introduces a new class of trust problem: natural-language user intent passes through autonomous shopping agents, merchant agents, and credential providers before it is expressed as cryptographic payment artifacts. Existing protocols — AP2, Verifiable Intent (VI), and UCP — secure the lower layers (mandate delegation, checkout authorisation, and payment completion) but do not define a standard protocol for an independent assessor to retrieve and assess the pre-checkout intent interpretation and agent reasoning that produced those artifacts.

ATLAS fills this gap. It defines a portable, cryptographically verifiable protocol through which assessor (for instance agent accredited by a certification body (CB)) may obtain scoped, encrypted evidence from a shopping agent — without requiring trust in any individual intermediary and without revealing sensitive user intent to parties with no legitimate need.

1.1 Problem Statement§

Autonomous AI agents are increasingly authorised to act on behalf of human principals in commerce, finance, and regulated industries — selecting goods, negotiating terms, and initiating payments without per-transaction human approval. This autonomy introduces a systemic oversight gap: the agent's chain of decisions between the user's original instruction and the final payment action is opaque, probabilistic, and subject to silent drift. No party in the value chain — merchant, credential provider, payment network, issuer, or regulator — currently holds a standardised, independently verifiable record of what the agent understood and acted upon.

For the payment industry this gap is acute. Schemes and issuers require trust signals that travel with the authorisation message and can be used to:

  • Mitigate chargeback risk. Distinguish legitimate agent-initiated purchases from fraudulent ones, and provide an auditable record of authorised user intent that survives a "I did not authorise this" dispute.
  • Manage fraud liability. Determine whether liability shifts to the agent provider, merchant, or issuer when an agent-initiated transaction is later disputed, based on independent evidence of what the agent was instructed to do versus what it actually did.
  • Ensure compliance. Demonstrate that agent-initiated transactions comply with applicable regulations — including the EU AI Act (transparency and explainability obligations for high-risk AI systems), PSD2 / PSD3 (strong customer authentication and transaction monitoring), DORA (operational resilience and auditability of third-party AI systems), and the Interchange Fee Regulation (IFR, EU 2015/751) (cost justification and routing transparency for card-based transactions where agent-selected routing may affect interchange) — as well as network rulebooks, at the time of authorisation and not only at onboarding.
  • Protect data privacy. Enable auditability of agent reasoning without exposing raw user data to payment networks, merchants, or processors: the trust signal must be verifiable without being a surveillance instrument.
  • Avoid disputes. Give merchants and consumers a common reference record — signed and time-stamped by a neutral party — that can be consulted before a chargeback is filed and used to resolve disputes without litigation.
  • Extend and continuously feed KYA monitoring. Know Your Agent (KYA) frameworks perform pre-runtime identity verification — confirming who built the agent, which cryptographic keys it holds, and what scopes it claims at registration. ATLAS extends this static identity layer with runtime behavioural evidence: every AuditResult and associated evidence hash constitutes a machine-readable behavioural signal that can be fed back into KYA monitoring pipelines. Over time, a series of AuditResult verdicts for a given agent identifier reveals drift patterns — scope creep, intent distortion, or policy violations — that are invisible to static KYA checks but detectable through continuous audit. Payment networks and issuers can therefore use ATLAS-derived signals to update agent trust scores, trigger re-attestation requirements, or suspend agent credentials when behavioural deviations cross a defined threshold, creating a closed loop between initial agent identity verification and ongoing runtime trustworthiness assessment.

Existing protocols address complementary problems. AP2 and Verifiable Intent (VI) provide strong cryptographic controls once the flow is expressed as mandates and payment artifacts, but do not define a standard protocol for an independent assessor to assess how the agent interpreted user intent, propagated constraints, and selected merchants before those artifacts were created. Know Your Agent (KYA) frameworks establish pre-runtime identity — who built the agent and which keys it holds — but cannot guarantee what the agent actually did during a live transaction.

Intent drift is the primary and most operationally significant instance of this broader oversight gap. It occurs when the agent's interpretation of natural-language user constraints (budget, allergens, ethical preferences, risk appetite, medical limitations) diverges from the user's actual intent across the interpretation, structuring, and presentation layers of an agentic commerce stack (see §1.4). In the payment context, intent drift translates directly into wrong purchases, misallocated liability, and undetected fraud — with no independent record to adjudicate the outcome.

ATLAS standardises how authorized assessor obtain bounded and encrypted evidence from the shopping agent, evaluate it against a named policy referential, and produce a compact cryptographic trust signal (CBReportToken) that can be carried in ISO 8583, EMV 3DS, and ISO 20022 messages.

1.2 Design Goals§

  1. Privacy by default. Structured summaries; encrypt user intent and agent reasoning so only the designated assessor can read it.
  2. Cryptographic chain of trust. Verifiable chain across shopping agent, assessor, merchant, CP, processor, network, bank, and certification body.
  3. Complementarity with AP2/VI/UCP. Align with mandate delegation, key binding, verifier receipts — don't replace them.
  4. Durable evidence. Support real-time processing decisions and later dispute review.
  5. Direct audit commissioning. Shopping agent may self-commission audits without a merchant holder.
  6. Scheme-level assessor roster. Discover eligible assessor, CB trust anchors, and accreditation metadata without ad hoc configuration.
  7. Separation of authority. Separate authority to request an audit / read private evidence / redeem downstream reports.
  8. Rail-compatible token. Downstream token fits in ISO 8583 / EMV 3DS / ISO 20022.
  9. Chained signed-token model. JWS/JWT + JWE over HTTPS/TLS.
  10. Chained capability tokens (cnf per RFC 7800) - (no human verifier in this flow).

1.4 Why Audit? The Agentic Commerce Trust Problem§

The Three Risk Layers in Agentic Commerce

An agentic commerce flow converts a human's natural-language expression into a structured payment action — choosing merchants, building carts, and initiating checkout — without the human reviewing every intermediate step. That conversion passes through three consecutive layers, each of which can silently distort the user's original intent:

Interpretation layer.
The large language model (LLM) maps the user's free text to an internal representation using the probability distributions encoded in its weights. Constraints that are rare, ambiguous, or medically nuanced (e.g. a specific allergen interaction with a prescribed drug) may be approximated, under-weighted, or collapsed into a generic label.
Structuring layer.
To invoke tools via MCP or a comparable protocol the LLM must serialise its interpretation into a typed, schema-constrained JSON object. Fields that do not map cleanly to the target schema are compressed or silently dropped. A constraint that survived the interpretation layer may not survive serialisation.
Filtering and presentation layer.
Privacy and safety filters (PII scrubbing, safety classifiers, content moderation) operate between the LLM and downstream systems. These layers are necessary but can unintentionally redact or generalise critical constraints — for example, a medical allergy note — before the tool call or the confirmation screen is rendered. The user then sees a simplified summary that looks correct while missing nuances that matter for safety or compliance.

Primary illustrative example — intent with health constraints

A user says: "I want to order a pizza. I am severely allergic to peanuts, I am on a particular heart medication, and my budget is 40 €."

The agent may encode the budget constraint very cleanly ("max_price": 40) because it fits neatly into every commerce schema it has seen during training. The allergy and the drug-interaction constraint, however, may only survive as a vague free-text annotation — or be stripped entirely by a PII filter — before the restaurant_search or add_to_cart MCP call is issued. The confirmation screen then presents "vegetarian pizza, 40 €": visually reasonable, yet missing the critical safety constraints the user expressed. If the agent places an order that causes harm, no party in the value chain holds an auditable record of what the agent actually understood and acted upon.

From KYA to Continuous Runtime Audit

Payment networks have responded to agentic commerce by introducing Know Your Agent (KYA) frameworks — agent registration, cryptographic key binding, and verifiable credentials that attest to an agent's identity before any token is issued. KYA is a necessary foundation, but it is pre-runtime: it establishes who built the agent and which keys it holds, not what the agent actually does during a live transaction.

Modern shopping agents are non-deterministic systems subject to prompt injection, memory poisoning, supply-chain compromise, and non-deterministic drift: identical prompts can produce different outputs across runs, and the agent's effective behaviour can change after a model update or tool swap without any visible modification to its KYA credentials. A clean cryptographic handshake at checkout does not reveal whether the agent silently dropped a medical constraint five steps earlier.

ATLAS addresses this gap by providing a neutral runtime trust layer that continuously verifies what agents actually do, sitting above payment rails and below agents, independently of any single network or agent provider.

What the assessor Evaluates: Agent Data vs. Referential

an assessor agent evaluates a structured evidence package produced by the shopping agent against a named policy referential (policy_referential claim in AuditDelegation). The evidence package — transmitted as a private JWE, never forwarded to the merchant or payment network — contains:

  • User intent data. The natural-language expression, intent summary, and structured constraint set as interpreted by the shopping agent at the start of the session.
  • Reasoning chain. The agent's step-by-step reasoning that led from the original intent to the selected merchant(s) and SKUs — including any constraint that was evaluated, narrowed, or deprioritised.
  • Supply-chain data. Tool provenance (which MCP servers / plugins were invoked), model version, and any retrieved catalogue or RAG context that influenced the selection.
  • Transaction artifacts. References to checkout objects and AP2 mandate fields for cross-checking.

The policy referential identifies the framework or rulebook against which the evidence is evaluated. Examples:

Referential typeExampleAudit question
Regulatory EU AI Act Article 13 transparency requirements Did the agent disclose its automated nature and the constraints it applied?
Network rulebook Card payment network - Trusted Agent Policy Did the agent stay within registered scopes and spend limits?
Industry standard OWASP LLM Top 10 — Prompt Injection prevention Is there evidence that the agent's reasoning was injected or manipulated?
Health and safety Food allergen disclosure obligations (EU 1169/2011) Were declared allergen constraints propagated to every item selection call?
Merchant policy Age-restricted goods verification rule Did the agent verify user eligibility before adding restricted items to cart?

The assessor produces an AuditResult (Link 4) containing a binary or graded verdict, a hash of the evidence reviewed, the policy_referential applied, and — when the certification body so requires — a cb_report_token that can be redeemed by the payment network or issuer for a certified trust-assurance report. This is the mechanism by which a payment authorisation decision can incorporate independent runtime evidence about agent behaviour, complementing the static KYA signals already present in the authorisation message.

3. Terminology§

Agentic Commerce
A commerce flow in which autonomous AI agents negotiate, select, and authorise transactions on behalf of a user using structured mandate and policy artifacts.
Shopping Agent
An autonomous software agent that interprets user intent, discovers offers, builds carts, and serves as root of trust for user intent and reasoning evidence within ATLAS.
Holder
The merchant or credential provider that holds an ATLAS AuditDelegation and may issue a chained AuditSubDelegation. Holder mode only.
Assessor Agent
An accredited software agent that presents an AuditAccessAssertion, receives encrypted evidence, evaluates it, and issues signed AuditResult and CBReportToken.
Certification Body (CB)
An independent organisation that accredits assessor agents, signs accreditation credentials, stores certified reports, and redeems CBReportToken values.
Certification Scheme
A governance framework that manages participating CBs and publishes the authoritative assessor roster.
AuditDelegation (Link 1)
Signed JWT issued by the shopping agent to a merchant/CP (holder mode) or directly to an assessor (direct mode). Root of the ATLAS capability chain.
AuditSubDelegation (Link 2)
Signed JWT issued by a holder to a specific assessor, chained to Link 1 via parent_jti, binding the assessor's key in cnf. Holder mode only.
AuditAccessAssertion (Link 3)
Signed JWS issued by the assessor to the shopping agent, embedding the delegation chain and accreditation credential, signed over a fresh nonce. Unlocks the JWE evidence release on successful verification.
AuditEvidenceResponse
JWE (RFC 7516) issued by the shopping agent to the assessor containing sensitive user intent and agent reasoning. Not forwarded to any other party.
AuditResult (Link 4)
Signed JWS result envelope issued by the assessor containing the audit verdict, policy referential, evidence hashes, and a cb_report_token.
CBReportToken (Link 5)
Compact, opaque downstream token providing a redeemable reference to one certified audit report. One token per audit.
redeem_tokens
Payment-rail-only field (ISO 8583 / EMV 3DS / ISO 20022) assembled by the merchant processor from up to three cb_report_token values. Not an ATLAS protocol link.
cnf claim
JSON key confirmation claim (RFC 7800) binding a specific public key via JWK thumbprint (jkt) to a JWT.
audit_mode
Claim on AuditDelegation: holder (Pattern A) or direct (Pattern B).
policy_referential
Identifier for the audit framework or regulatory standard against which the assessor evaluates evidence.
Assessor Roster
Machine-readable catalogue published by a certification scheme, listing every currently accredited assessor agent with its public keys, audit types, regions, and owning CB.
Hyperlab
The Innovation Lab within Consult Hyperion responsible for this specification and related ATLAS artefacts as part of the Continuous Certification for Agentic Commerce programme.

5. assessor Roster§

Each certification scheme MUST expose an assessor roster — a machine-readable, periodically updated catalogue of every assessor agent currently accredited by any CB associated with that scheme. The roster is the canonical discovery surface for ATLAS participants.

The roster is used for discovery and pre-filtering. The live accreditation credential presented at assertion time (§8.4) is the authoritative eligibility check.

5.1 Scope§

One scheme roster aggregates assessors from all CBs linked to the scheme. Shopping agents, merchants, and credential providers use a single scheme endpoint to learn who may perform which audit types in which regions.

5.2 Per-assessor Entry Fields§

5.3 Publication and Trust§

The scheme SHOULD sign roster snapshots or publish over HTTPS with a scheme trust anchor, and SHOULD support versioning (roster_version, updated_at) and revocation. Implementations MAY expose the roster as a JSON document, REST collection, or signed JWT bundle.

5.4 Use in ATLAS Flows§

  • Shopping agent MAY copy jkt values from roster entries into permitted_assessors / permitted_certification_bodies when building Link 1.
  • In holder mode, the merchant or CP selects an assessor from the roster and binds the assessor's jkt in Link 2 cnf.
  • Holders and assessors SHOULD reject commission if the assessor's keys or accreditation dates do not match the current roster entry.

7. Audit Initiation Patterns§

7.1 Pattern A — Holder Mode§

The shopping agent issues AuditDelegation to a merchant or CP during negotiation. The holder selects an eligible assessor and issues a chained AuditSubDelegation. Each delegation is independent — the merchant and CP may each commission different assessors.

7.2 Pattern B — Direct Mode§

The shopping agent commissions an audit for its own behaviour — for self-compliance, regulatory evidence, or pre-dispute attestation. There is no merchant and no Link 2.

In direct mode the shopping agent MUST NOT be the sole source of assessor trust. permitted_assessors / permitted_certification_bodies MUST be constrained by scheme roster and scheme or enterprise policy to prevent the SA from unilaterally appointing a captive assessor.

8. Protocol Steps§

All interactions are between server-side agents over HTTPS with server TLS. There is no human in the ATLAS protocol loop. Application trust is entirely in the signed-token chain.

8.1 Step 1 — Audit Delegation Issuance (Holder Mode)§

The shopping agent issues two independent AuditDelegation tokens during negotiation — one to the merchant (bound to merchant key and checkout artifacts) and one to the CP (bound to CP key and payment artifacts).

8.2 Step 1b — Direct Mode Commission§

audit_mode MUST be direct; sub MUST be the assessor identifier; cnf.jkt MUST be the assessor's public key. No merchant cnf and no Link 2.

8.3 Step 2 — Sub-Delegation and Audit Commission (Holder Mode Only)§

The holder issues a chained AuditSubDelegation, signed with the holder's key matching the parent cnf, that names the assessor as sub, binds the assessor's key in cnf, references the parent via parent_jti, and narrows or equals the parent scope.

  1. Verify shopping-agent signature on parent token (iss, exp, nbf, jti).
  2. Verify holder signature on sub-delegation matches parent cnf.
  3. Verify merchant identity matches parent sub.
  4. Verify own signing key matches sub-delegation cnf.
  5. Verify eligibility under permitted_assessors / permitted_certification_bodies and optional filters.
  6. Verify sub-delegation scope ⊆ parent scope.
  7. Verify replay protection on jti.

8.4 Step 3 — Access Assertion to Shopping Agent§

The assessor sends an AuditAccessAssertion — a signed JWS wrapping the delegation chain plus accreditation, signed over a fresh nonce from the shopping agent.

Holder-mode verification checklist

CheckFail closed if
Parent AuditDelegation: audit_mode: holder, valid signature, exp/nbf, jtiInvalid, expired, revoked, or wrong mode
AuditSubDelegation signature matches parent cnfHolder key mismatch
Sub-delegation sub and cnf match assertion signerWrong subject or key
assessor eligible per permitted_assessors / permitted_certification_bodiesNot eligible
Sub-delegation scope ⊆ parent scopeScope widened
Accreditation valid for scope and regionUntrusted or expired
Assertion signature over nonce; nonce not previously usedReplay detected

Scope for JWE release: sub-delegation scope (holder) / Link 1 scope (direct).

8.5 Step 4 — Evidence Response§

  1. Build JSON payload scoped per Link 2 scope (holder) or Link 1 scope (direct), filtered server-side before encryption.
  2. Encrypt to assessor public key in Link 2 cnf (holder) or Link 1 cnf (direct).
  3. Algorithms: ECDH-ES or RSA-OAEP for key encryption; A256GCM for content encryption.
  4. Optionally nest a SA-signed inner JWS inside the JWE for combined confidentiality and integrity.
  5. No other party receives this JWE — not the merchant, CP, network, or CB.

Minimum payload: user's natural-language intent + agent reasoning leading to merchant/item selection. Additional categories when in scope: intent_summary, constraint_set, merchant_selection_reasoning, item_selection_reasoning, policy_evaluation_results, evidence_hashes.

8.6 Step 5 — Audit Result Issuance§

AuditResult is addressed to the merchant/CP (holder mode) or to the shopping agent (direct mode). The requester MUST convey the full signed AuditResult peer-to-peer until the merchant processor holds every result for that transaction_reference.

8.7 Step 6 — Downstream Report Redemption§

The merchant processor collects conveyed AuditResult payloads, extracts cb_report_token from each, and writes an ordered redeem_tokens list (max 3 entries) into the payment message. The CB MUST reject redemption outside [redeem_nbf, redeem_exp] and MAY enforce one-time redemption via jti tracking.

11. Security and Privacy Requirements§

11.1 Security Design Principles§

PrincipleHow ATLAS applies it
Separation of dutiesL1 authorises commissioning; L3+JWE authorises reading evidence; L5/redeem_tokens authorises downstream redemption only.
Least privilegeSub-delegation scope MUST NOT exceed parent. Evidence filtered server-side before JWE.
Fail closedAny signature, expiry, scope, eligibility, or binding check failure → no evidence released.
Cryptographic bindingcnf/jkt binds each link to the next signer; transaction_reference and audit_id bind to a transaction.
Confidentiality vs integrityJWE for evidence; JWS/JWT for delegation and results.
Defence in depthRoster for discovery + live CB accreditation at assertion time + SA verification before JWE.

11.2 Normative Controls§

  • All delegation and result artifacts MUST use asymmetric signatures (e.g. ES256, EdDSA). MUST prohibit none and deprecated HMAC-as-JWT.
  • Every JWT MUST include exp, nbf, and unique jti. Verifiers MUST reject expired or reused jti.
  • aud and iss MUST be validated on every presentation.
  • Link 2 scope MUST be a subset of Link 1 scope.
  • The SA MUST issue a fresh, single-use nonce for each AuditAccessAssertion request.
  • Any agent receiving a conveyed AuditResult MUST verify the assessor signature, transaction_reference, audit_id, and requester_id.
  • The merchant processor MUST NOT mint tokens or accept results without the signed envelope.

11.3 Privacy§

  • User intent and agent reasoning MUST use JWE to Link 2/1 cnf only.
  • Merchants, CPs, and CBs MUST NOT receive AuditEvidenceResponse or decrypted evidence in the base profile.
  • Do not log decrypted evidence in production; log audit_id and hashes only.

11.4 Transport§

HTTPS with TLS 1.2+ (1.3 preferred); certificate validation enabled. Application trust does not rely on mTLS or DPoP in the base profile.

11.5 Downstream Redemption — Accepted Model and Risks§

RiskMitigation
Token copied from payment logsMinimise logging; short redeem_exp
Insider injects bogus tokensProcessor MUST only copy from verified AuditResult; scheme monitoring
Replay of redeemed reportCB MUST track jti for one-time redemption
Weak assurance vs agent-layer cryptoHigh-assurance schemes MAY require signed redemption JWT or mTLS at CB API

11.6 Threat-Model Checklist (Implementers)§

  • ☐ Compromised merchant issues L2 to colluding assessor → contained by SA verification at §8.4 and permitted_* on L1.
  • ☐ Compromised assessor requests excessive scope → contained by scope ⊆ parent and SA filter before JWE.
  • ☐ Compromised SA over-releases evidence → contained by scope; inner JWS + CB audit trail.
  • ☐ Replay of assertion or delegation → jti, nonce, short exp.
  • ☐ Forged conveyed AuditResult → mandatory signature verification at processor and peers.
  • ☐ Stale roster / revoked assessor → accreditation credential + optional revocation list.

12. Alignment with AP2, Verifiable Intent, and UCP§

ATLAS is complementary to AP2: AP2 defines mandate delegation, action authorisation, verifier checks, and receipts; ATLAS adds a standard path for auditing how the shopping agent interpreted and operationalised the user's intent before and during those actions.

ATLAS borrows the principles of Verifiable Intent — key-bound credentials, audience restriction, chained authority — but uses chained capability tokens (JWS/JWT with cnf per RFC 7800) rather than W3C VC/VP envelopes. There is no human verifier in the loop, so selective-disclosure cryptography and credential portability are not needed. Implementations that already deploy VI MAY wrap ATLAS tokens inside VI presentations at payment boundaries.

UCP defines the end-to-end commerce lifecycle — from offer negotiation through checkout completion. ATLAS attaches to that lifecycle at two points: during negotiation, where an AuditResult may influence offer terms, and at completion, where the CBReportToken travels with the payment authorisation message. The FACT implementation has already demonstrated that merchant-to-assessor invocation works within UCP's negotiation phase; ATLAS formalises that proven pattern into a portable, scheme-agnostic protocol.

13. Proposed Standard Text Seed§

This section is non-normative.

ATLAS defines a chained-capability-token audit framework for agentic commerce with two initiation patterns. A certification scheme publishes an assessor roster listing every accredited assessor agent from all CBs under the scheme, including assessor and CB public keys (jkt), audit types, regions, accreditation dates, and CB association.

In holder mode, the shopping agent issues AuditDelegation (audit_mode: holder) to the merchant and CP; a holder commissions an audit via AuditSubDelegation to an eligible assessor. In direct mode, the shopping agent issues AuditDelegation (audit_mode: direct) straight to an assessor — no merchant and no Link 2. In both modes the assessor presents an AuditAccessAssertion; the SA verifies the chain and returns AuditEvidenceResponse as JWE. The assessor returns AuditResult with one cb_report_token. Each result is conveyed peer-to-peer until the merchant processor collects them, extracts cb_report_token values (up to three), and writes redeem_tokens on ISO 8583 and similar rails. Application trust is chained JWS/JWT plus JWE over HTTPS with standard TLS.
